## 15. AKASHIWO SANGUINEA — SEABIRD FOAM MORTALITY

*Mechanism: A. sanguinea produces proteinaceous mycosporine-like amino acids (MAAs) that act as surfactants, generating stable sea foam when bloom biomass is lysed by wave action. This foam saturates seabird plumage, destroying waterproofing and causing fatal hypothermia. No ichthyotoxin is involved — purely a physical surfactant mechanism unique in the HAB literature.*

### 15.1 Founding event — Monterey Bay 2007

**Jessup DA, Miller MA, Ryan JP, Nevins HM, Kerkering HA, Mekebri A, Crane DB, Johnson TA, Kudela RM** (2009). Mass stranding of marine birds caused by a surfactant-producing red tide. *PLoS ONE* 4(2):e4550.
- DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0004550 | PMCID: PMC2641015
- **Open access: YES (CC0 Public Domain)**
- **event_id suggested: USA_2007_001**
- *November–December 2007, Northeastern Monterey Bay, California. First documented case of A. sanguinea foam killing seabirds. 700+ marine birds affected across 14 species; severe hypothermia from feather waterproofing loss. A. sanguinea foam confirmed by spectrometry; no conventional toxins (domoic acid, saxitoxin, brevetoxin) detected. Causative mechanism fully established.*

### 15.2 Washington/Oregon coast 2009 — largest event

**Jones T, Parrish JK, Punt AE, Trainer VL, Kudela R, Lang J, Brancato MS, Odell A, Hickey B** (2017). Mass mortality of marine birds in the Northeast Pacific caused by *Akashiwo sanguinea*. *Marine Ecology Progress Series* 579:111–127.
- DOI: 10.3354/meps12253
- **Open access: NO — PDF upload needed**
- **event_id suggested: USA_2009_001**
- *September–November 2009, Washington and Oregon coast. Two mortality events totalling an estimated 10,500 carcasses deposited on Washington shores. Majority surf scoters (*Melanitta perspicillata*), white-winged scoters (*M. deglandi*), and common murres (*Uria aalge*). Synchronous bloom along OR-WA coast; bloom peak coincided with primary moult of overwintering seabirds. This study is the largest seabird mortality event definitively attributed to a single HAB.*

### 15.3 Large-scale 2009 bloom ecology

**Du X, Peterson W, Fisher J, Hunter M, Peterson J** (2011). Initiation and development of a toxic and persistent *Akashiwo sanguinea* bloom in the northern California current system in 2009. *Harmful Algae* 10(4):354–369.
- DOI: 10.1016/j.hal.2011.01.001
- **Open access: NO — PDF upload needed**
- *Large-scale bloom dynamics paper for the 2009 OR-WA event. Provides bloom initiation, extent, oceanographic context, and spatial heterogeneity data relevant to the USA_2009_001 catalog entry.*

### 15.4 Background — seabirds and HABs (review)

**WHOI National Office for Harmful Algal Blooms** (2024). Birds — Harmful Algal Blooms [web resource].
- URL: https://hab.whoi.edu/impacts/impacts-wildlife/hab-impacts-on-birds/
- **Open access: YES**
- *Comprehensive overview of all HAB-seabird interactions including PSP (Alexandrium in Alaskan seabirds), brevetoxin (K. brevis pelican deaths, Florida), and Akashiwo foam events. Useful for contextualising these events within the broader HAB-wildlife framework.*

## 16. AUREOCOCCUS ANOPHAGEFFERENS — NORTHEAST USA BROWN TIDES

*Mechanism: High-biomass blooms (>10⁶ cells/mL) cause extreme light attenuation, starving seagrasses and bivalves. A. anophagefferens is not directly toxic; harm is biomass-mediated. Taxonomic class: Pelagophyceae (not covered by existing schema enum "Other" is appropriate). First major Pelagophyceae representative in the catalog.*

### 16.1 Founding review

**Bricelj VM, Lonsdale DJ** (1997). *Aureococcus anophagefferens*: Causes and ecological consequences of brown tides in US mid-Atlantic coastal waters. *Limnology and Oceanography* 42(5pt2):1023–1038.
- DOI: 10.4319/lo.1997.42.5_part_2.1023
- **Open access: NO — PDF upload needed**
- **event_id suggested: USA_1985_001**
- *Definitive review of brown tide events 1985–1996. Long Island bay scallop (*Argopecten irradians*) industry collapsed: landings dropped from 300,000 lbs/yr average to 300 lbs in 1987–88. Mussels suffered 30–100% mortality in Narragansett Bay 1985. Seagrass (*Zostera marina*, *Halodule wrightii*) loss confirmed. Economic loss of bay scallop industry estimated at $2M/year (Kahn and Rockel, 1988 via NCCOS). Brown tides spread from Rhode Island to Virginia by 1990s.*

### 16.2 Original species description

**Sieburth JM, Johnson PW, Hargraves PE** (1988). Ultrastructure and ecology of *Aureococcus anophagefferens* gen. et sp. nov. (Chrysophyceae): The dominant picoplankter during a bloom in Narragansett Bay, Rhode Island, Summer 1985. *Journal of Phycology* 24:416–425.
- DOI: 10.1111/j.1529-8817.1988.tb04483.x
- **Open access: NO — PDF upload needed**
- *Founding species description paper. First record of *A. anophagefferens* from 1985 Narragansett Bay bloom. Note: originally classified in Chrysophyceae, now placed in Pelagophyceae.*

### 16.3 Ecosystem disruptive blooms — comprehensive synthesis

**Gobler CJ, Sunda WG** (2012). Ecosystem disruptive algal blooms of the brown tide species, *Aureococcus anophagefferens* and *Aureoumbra lagunensis*. *Harmful Algae* 14:360–374.
- DOI: 10.1016/j.hal.2011.10.013
- **Open access: NO — PDF upload needed**
- *Mechanistic and ecological synthesis of both brown tide species. Addresses nutrient physiology enabling bloom persistence under low-nutrient conditions. Describes ecological cascade: brown tide → zooplankton decline → bivalve starvation → seagrass loss → altered benthic community.*

### 16.4 Range expansion to South Africa and China

**Anderson DM, Fensin E, Gobler CJ, et al.** (2021). Marine harmful algal blooms (HABs) in the United States: History, current status and future trends. *Harmful Algae* 102:101975.
- DOI: 10.1016/j.hal.2021.101975 [already in catalog as reference, new extraction for Aureococcus events]
- *Documents expansion of *A. anophagefferens* from northeastern USA to Delaware/Maryland/Virginia, South Africa, and northern China (Bohai Sea). Confirms ecosystem-disrupting status across three continents.*

## 16B. AUREOUMBRA LAGUNENSIS — TEXAS LAGUNA MADRE BROWN TIDE

*Mechanism: Same as Aureococcus; extreme light attenuation; not directly toxic. Unique in that it caused the longest continuous phytoplankton bloom ever documented globally.*

### 16B.1 Primary documentation

**Buskey EJ, Wysor B, Hyatt C** (1998). The role of hypersalinity in the persistence of the Texas "brown tide" in the Laguna Madre. *Journal of Plankton Research* 20(7):1553–1565.
- DOI: 10.1093/plankt/20.7.1553
- **Open access: NO — PDF upload needed**
- **event_id suggested: USA_1990_001**
- *January 1990 to late 1997 — 7+ years continuous bloom of *A. lagunensis* in Laguna Madre/Baffin Bay, Texas. Bloom densities 0.5–5 × 10⁶ cells/mL. Loss of *Halodule wrightii* seagrass beds; decline of benthic invertebrate communities. Described as the longest continuous phytoplankton bloom in recorded history. Note: bloom ended when above-normal rainfall in fall 1997 reduced hypersaline conditions.*

### 16B.2 Range expansion to Florida 2012

**Gobler CJ, Koch F, Kang YJ, Berry DL, Tang YZ, Lasi M, Walters L, Hall L, Miller JD** (2013). Expansion of harmful brown tides caused by the pelagophyte, *Aureoumbra lagunensis* DeYoe et Stockwell, to the US East Coast. *Harmful Algae* 27:29–41.
- DOI: 10.1016/j.hal.2013.04.004
- **Open access: NO — PDF upload needed**
- **event_id suggested: USA_2012_001**
- *Summer 2012 bloom of *A. lagunensis* in Indian River Lagoon and Mosquito Lagoon, Florida. Chlorophyll-a reached ~200 µg/L (20× historical mean). Near-hypoxic conditions and >30 fish kill reports in Mosquito Lagoon in September 2012. Genetically confirmed as *A. lagunensis*. Represents first range expansion outside Texas.*

## 17. SEA OF MARMARA MUCILAGE 2020–2021

*Causative: Phaeocystis pouchetii (Prymnesiophyceae) dominant, with Skeletonema costatum, Cylindrotheca closterium, Thalassiosira rotula, and Gonyaulax fragilis co-occurring. Mucilage results from excessive extracellular polymer secretion triggered by eutrophication and stratification. The 2020–2021 event was the largest and most ecologically damaging mucilage event ever recorded globally.*

### 17.1 Primary scientific documentation

**Balkıs-Özdelice N, Durmuş T, Balcı M** (2021). A preliminary study on the intense pelagic and benthic mucilage phenomenon observed in the Sea of Marmara. *International Journal of Environment and Geoinformatics* 8(4):414–422.
- DOI: 10.30897/ijegeo.906946
- **Open access: YES (Turkish open-access journal)**
- **event_id suggested: TUR_2021_001**
- *First formal scientific publication on the 2021 event. Documents phytoplankton community, mucilage composition, spatial extent (>12,000 hectares surface; benthic coverage to 30 m depth), and preliminary ecological impacts. Primary causative organism identified as *Phaeocystis pouchetii*.*

### 17.2 Fisheries socioeconomic impact

**Yıldız T, Gönülal O** (2021). Sea snot and its impacts on the fisheries in the Sea of Marmara and its adjacent waters. *Journal of the Black Sea/Mediterranean Environment* 27(2):129–139.
- URL: https://blackmeditjournal.org/volumes-archive/vol-27-2021/vol-27-2021-no-2/
- **Open access: YES (journal is open access)**
- *Survey of 211 commercial and recreational fishers, May–June 2021. All components of fishing industry suffered monetary loss. "Impossible to fish" conditions. Tourism also severely impacted. Provides direct economic loss evidence meeting catalog threshold.*

### 17.3 Ecosystem and fish assemblage impacts

**Karadurmuş U, Sarı M** (2024). Post-mucilage teleost fish assemblages in the Sea of Marmara. [Title approximated from abstract — full citation in search results]. Available via ResearchGate DOI.
- **event_id: linked to TUR_2021_001**
- *Bottom trawl surveys at 52 stations across Sea of Marmara in 2023. Species richness and total teleost biomass decreased by >20% in the post-mucilage period compared to pre-mucilage baseline. Smelt, anchovy, and picarel particularly impacted. Confirms long-term ecosystem damage beyond the acute 2021 event.*

### 17.4 Ecological context and action plan

**Turkish Marine Research Foundation (TUDAV)** (2021). Press Release — Sea Snot (Mucilage) Update [press release and action plan summary].
- URL: https://tudav.org/en/from-us/press-releases/press-release-sea-snot-mucilage-update/
- **Open access: YES (government/NGO document)**
- *Documents the government 22-point action plan, declaration of Marmara as marine protected area, and key facts: mucilage extent, benthic coverage, affected species, and the previous 2007–2009 mucilage events (showing recurrence pattern). Notes that "one fifth of all plants and animals died in the Marmara Sea" during the 2021 event.*

- **Country:** Turkey (TUR)
- **Water body:** Sea of Marmara / Bosphorus Strait / northern Aegean Sea
- **Environment:** estuarine / marine_coastal

## 18. PRYMNESIUM PARVUM — ODER RIVER DISASTER 2022 (GERMANY/POLAND)

*The largest freshwater fish kill caused by a HAB ever documented in Europe. Prymnesium parvum grew to >100 million cells/L in the river, enabled by artificially elevated salinity from industrial/mining discharge combined with low water levels and high temperatures during a summer heatwave.*

### 18.1 Primary peer-reviewed analysis

**Absalon D, Matysik M, Krynicka J, Kozłowski K** (2023). Insights into toxic *Prymnesium parvum* blooms as a cause of the ecological disaster on the Odra River. *Toxins* 15(6):403.
- DOI: 10.3390/toxins15060403 | PMCID: PMC10302719
- **Open access: YES (MDPI CC BY)**
- **event_id suggested: POL_2022_001 and DEU_2022_001** (multi-country event)
- *July–September 2022. Fish mortality in five Polish provinces (Silesia, Opole, Lower Silesia, Lubuskie, Western Pomerania) along 742 km of the Oder/Odra River. Multiple species killed; 50% decline in fish population; histopathology showed acute gill, kidney, and spleen damage from hemolytic prymnesins. B-type Prymnesium parvum identified by molecular methods. Maximum conductivity >2,000 µS/cm and pH 8+ confirmed bloom-facilitating conditions. IGB (Leibniz Institute) estimate: up to 1,000 tonnes fish killed. Wikipedia cites "approximately 360 tonnes" as confirmed; IGB public statement says "up to 1,000 tonnes."*

### 18.2 IGB research programme factsheet

**Leibniz Institute of Freshwater Ecology and Inland Fisheries (IGB)** (2022/2024). Oder River disaster: What is known about the alga *Prymnesium parvum*? [IGB factsheet and policy brief, ongoing ODER~SO research program].
- URL: https://www.igb-berlin.de/en/news/oder-river-disaster-what-known-about-alga-prymnesium-parvum
- **Open access: YES (government research institute)**
- *IGB factsheet documents P. parvum B-type genotype identification, bloom densities (>100 million cells/L in Gliwice Canal), ecological context, and management implications. Policy brief calls for reduced industrial salt discharge into river. Confirms species established in Oder system post-2022.*

### 18.3 Prymnesin toxin profiling during the bloom

**Mora D, Schlüsener M, Fischer H, Kleinteich J, et al.** (2024). From genes to toxins: Profiling *Prymnesium parvum* during a riverine harmful algal bloom. *Harmful Algae* 136:102644.
- DOI: 10.1016/j.hal.2024.102644 | PMID: 38876525
- **Open access: NO — PDF upload needed**
- *PCR/eDNA detection and prymnesin quantification directly from Oder River water samples. Confirms high P. parvum and prymnesin concentrations at time of ecological disaster. Provides D. magna 90% mortality in 24h bioassay data, confirming toxicity to invertebrates beyond fish.*

### Key event data for POL_2022_001 / DEU_2022_001:
- **Years:** 2022
- **Countries:** Poland (POL) and Germany (DEU) — create two entries with shared event_name
- **Water body:** Oder River (Odra) — Poland/Germany border river
- **Environment type:** `brackish` (artificially salinised river)
- **Species primary:** *Prymnesium parvum* (Prymnesiophyceae)
- **fish_affected_type:** wild
- **fish_killed_tonnes:** 360–1000 (use 360 as minimum, 1000 as high estimate; qualifier: minimum)
- **fish_killed_n:** hundreds of thousands (qualifier: estimated)
- **Ecological impact:** ecosystem_wide (fish, mussels, aquatic snails, zooplankton, amphibians)
- **data_quality:** high (peer-reviewed Toxins paper + IGB factsheet)
- **Notes:** "First catastrophic freshwater Prymnesium kill in Central Europe; enabled by mining-derived salinisation of river"

## 20. CYANOTOXIN EVENTS — FRESHWATER/DRINKING WATER/DIALYSIS

### 20.1 Brazil Caruaru 1996 — dialysis deaths

**Jochimsen EM, Carmichael WW, An JS, Cardo DM, Cookson ST, Holmes CE, Antunes MB, de Melo Filho DA, Lyra TM, Barreto VS, Azevedo SM, Jarvis WR** (1998). Liver failure and death after exposure to microcystins at a hemodialysis center in Brazil. *New England Journal of Medicine* 338(13):873–878.
- DOI: 10.1056/NEJM199803263381304 | PMID: 9516222
- **Open access: YES (available via NEJM open archives)**
- **event_id suggested: BRA_1996_001**
- *Primary CDC/epidemiology paper. Dialysis center A in Caruaru, Pernambuco, Brazil. February 17–20, 1996: 116 of 130 patients developed visual disturbances, nausea, vomiting. 101 met case definition for acute liver failure; 50 died in initial study. All at center A; none at center B (which used treated, filtered, chlorinated water). Microcystins detected in reservoir water, clinic water, and patients' serum and liver tissue. No pesticides or other toxins found.*

**Pouria S, de Andrade A, Barbosa J, Cavalcanti RL, Barreto VT, Ward CJ, Preiser W, Poon GK, Neild GH, Codd GA** (1998). Fatal microcystin intoxication in haemodialysis unit in Caruaru, Brazil. *The Lancet* 352(9121):21–26.
- DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(97)12285-1 | PMID: 9800741
- **Open access: NO — PDF upload needed**
- *UK group's complementary report. All 126 patients developed acute neurotoxicity and subacute hepatotoxicity. 60 patients died. Histopathology: novel acute toxic hepatitis with diffuse panlobular hepatocyte necrosis. Microcystins confirmed in serum, dialysis filters, and water treatment columns.*

**Carmichael WW, Azevedo SM, An JS, Molica RJ, Jochimsen EM, Lau S, Rinehart KL, Shaw GR, Eaglesham GK** (2001). Human fatalities from cyanobacteria: Chemical and biological evidence for cyanotoxins. *Environmental Health Perspectives* 109(7):663–668.

**Azevedo SMFO, Carmichael WW, Jochimsen EM, Rinehart KL, Lau S, Shaw GR, Eaglesham GK** (2002). Human intoxication by microcystins during renal dialysis treatment in Caruaru–Brazil. *Toxicology* 181–182:441–446.

## 21. NODULARIA SPUMIGENA — BALTIC SEA ANNUAL BLOOMS

*Nodularia spumigena is the dominant producer of the hepatotoxin nodularin (pentapeptide, PP1/PP2A inhibitor) in the Baltic Sea. Blooms occur every summer, typically August–September, covering tens of thousands of km² across the entire Baltic basin. This is not a single catastrophic event but a chronic, recurrent, large-scale toxic bloom system.*

### 21.1 Founding chemical paper

**Sivonen K, Kononen K, Carmichael WW, Dahlem AM, Rinehart KL, Kiviranta J, Niemelä SI** (1989). Occurrence of the hepatotoxic cyanobacterium *Nodularia spumigena* in the Baltic Sea and the structure of the toxin. *Applied and Environmental Microbiology* 55(8):1990–1995.
- **Open access: YES (older ASM paper)** | PMID: 2506812
- **event_id suggested: BAL_1987_001** (first systematic documentation year, recurrent)
- *First formal documentation of toxic *N. spumigena* blooms and nodularin structure in the Baltic Sea. Establishes the brackish-water bloom system. Blooms documented from 1987 through the review period. Nodularin detected in water and cyanobacterial cells.*

### 21.2 Food web transfer and wildlife contamination

**Sipiä VO, Kankaanpää HT, Lahti K, Carmichael WW, Meriluoto JAO** (2001). Detection of nodularin in flounders and cod from the Baltic Sea. *Environmental Toxicology* 16(2):121–126.
- DOI: 10.1002/tox.1018

### 25.3 West Africa and East Africa

*After systematic searching, no peer-reviewed papers documenting specific catastrophic HAB events in West Africa (Senegal, Ghana, Nigeria, Côte d'Ivoire) or East Africa (Kenya, Tanzania, Mozambique) meeting the catalog threshold were identified. The peer-reviewed literature on HABs in these regions is limited to species occurrence records and potential risk assessments (Morocco/Morocco confirmed via MAR_1994_001 already in catalog). This represents a genuine literature gap reflecting under-reporting and limited monitoring capacity in the region, not absence of events. The HAEDAT database contains some low-quality occurrence records but none with the quantitative impact data required by the schema.*

**Reason for gap:** West and East African coastal nations have had little systematic HAB monitoring infrastructure until recently. IOC-UNESCO's efforts to build regional networks (FANSA — Framework for a Regional Network for Harmful Algae in Africa) are underway but data quality remains insufficient for catalog inclusion. This should be explicitly acknowledged in the manuscript methods section as a monitoring capacity limitation, not a true absence of events.

## SCHEMA NOTES AND RECOMMENDED VOCABULARY ADDITIONS

The freshwater/inland water events in this document (BRA_1996_001, AUS_1979_001, USA_2014_001, USA_2001_002, POL/DEU_2022_001, SWE_1987_001) reveal a schema gap:

**`environment_type` vocabulary should be extended to include:**
- `freshwater_lake` — Freshwater lake or reservoir
- `freshwater_river` — Freshwater river or stream

Without this, these events must use `brackish` (incorrect for Palm Island Solomon Dam, Toledo Lake Erie, or Caruaru reservoir) or be excluded. Recommend amending `controlled_vocabularies.json` and `event_catalog_schema.json` before extracting these entries.

**`human_syndrome` vocabulary should be extended to include:**
- `cyanotoxin` — Illness caused by any cyanobacterial toxin via any route (drinking water, dialysis, recreation)

This allows Caruaru, Palm Island, and Toledo to be classified consistently alongside PSP, DSP, etc.
